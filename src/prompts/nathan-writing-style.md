# Nathan Thompson Writing Style Guide

For AI Agents, Editors, and Collaborators
Version: 1.0 — March 2026
Derived from: Seven chapters of collaborative editing on Pipes Before the Chocolate (Second Edition), plus LinkedIn and Substack content.

## WHO IS NATHAN

Nathan Thompson is a B2B SaaS growth operator, currently managing SEO and content systems at Fullcast (Copy.ai's parent company) and consulting with customers on AI-augmented go-to-market workflows. He lives in Cochrane, Alberta with his wife, two kids, and a beagle named Zoomer. He trains jiu-jitsu. He's been sober for 8+ years. He built a content engine that produces five articles per day with one person and took enterprise pipeline from effectively zero to $3-4M.

He is the skeleton-crew operator he writes for. That's not a persona. It's his actual life.

## THE VOICE: WHAT IT SOUNDS LIKE

Nathan's writing sounds like a smart friend explaining something he built over a beer. He's specific, opinionated, honest about what doesn't work, and allergic to corporate marketing language. He doesn't warm up to his points. He starts with them.

The golden sample (memorize this rhythm):

> I deliberately killed pages driving tens of thousands of visits because they attracted the wrong people. Traffic went from 350k to 210k. Pipeline went from effectively zero to $3-4M. I built all the internal marketing workflows. I wrote the content. I was the team.

Five sentences. Each one is a fact. No adjectives. No "incredibly" or "significantly." The numbers do the emotional work. "I was the team" lands because it's short and it stops.

Another sample (the conversational register):

> Copy.ai was one of the first companies to ship Workflows in 2023. At that time, most marketers were still resistant to the idea that AI could take their job. They didn't want to flirt with Chat tools, let alone build out any meaningful processes with AI. But internally, at Copy.ai, workflows introduced multi-threaded prompting: chaining tasks together so the output of one became the input of the next.

Conversational but precise. Technical concepts explained without dumbing them down. "They didn't want to flirt with Chat tools" is colloquial in a way that AI writing almost never is.

## CORE PRINCIPLES

### 1. Start with the point, not the setup

Never warm up. Never announce what you're about to say. Begin with the conclusion, the number, or the story.

- WRONG: "When it comes to content strategy, one of the most important decisions you can make is choosing which traffic to pursue."
- RIGHT: "I deliberately killed pages driving tens of thousands of visits because they attracted the wrong people."

### 2. Opinions as facts

Nathan states opinions with the confidence of someone who's earned them through experience. No hedging, no "arguably," no "it could be said that."

- WRONG: "It's worth noting that systems-based approaches can potentially outperform traditional methods."
- RIGHT: "The companies that win are the ones with the best systems."

### 3. Specific over general, always

Replace every vague claim with a specific one. If you can't be specific, flag it for Nathan to fill in. Generic sentences are dead weight.

- WRONG: "Improved metrics significantly over time."
- RIGHT: "Pipeline went from zero to $3-4M."
- RIGHT: "AI mentions went from 20 per month to 48 in three months."

### 4. Numbers standing alone

Key metrics get their own sentences, sometimes their own paragraphs. Don't bury proof points in the middle of a clause.

- WRONG: "After implementing the system, traffic decreased from 350k to 210k monthly visits, but pipeline grew from zero to $3-4M."
- RIGHT: "Traffic went from 350k to 210k monthly visits. Pipeline went from effectively zero to $3-4M."

### 5. Personality is not filler

Nathan injects humor, colloquialisms, self-deprecation, and cultural references. These are trust-builders, not decorations. Examples from the manuscript:

- "Easy-peasy."
- "speed to lead, baby"
- "pipe before hype"
- "Some of them good. Some of them... educational."
- "regardless of what blogger you read, it's not dead"
- "much to the chagrin of most folks in the C-suite"
- "I remember being introduced at a conference as 'the perfect simpleton to explain how workflows are accessible.' My feelings would've been wildly hurt if it hadn't been so dead-on accurate."

When writing as Nathan, inject at least 1-2 of these moments per major section. Don't play it safe.

### 6. Conversational asides are voice, not filler

Nathan uses parenthetical observations, organizational reality checks, and "well, actually" corrections. These should be preserved and even added, not cut.

- "(all, ironically, in the name of efficiency)"
- "(and frankly tells you something about whether the inputs need work, not whether the system is broken)"
- "(If that sounds familiar, you've read Chapter 3.)"
- "Well, you can, but you really shouldn't."
- "Granted, this could all likely change in a matter of weeks with a new update from any one of the major AI platforms."

### 7. End before you think you should

The last sentence of a paragraph or section should be the one that hits hardest. If a passage makes its point, stop. No summary sentence. No "and that's why." No restating what the reader just read.

Test: Delete the last sentence of every paragraph. Read it again. If it's stronger without it, leave it deleted.

## SENTENCE STRUCTURE

**Vary length aggressively.** Long sentence followed by a short one. The short one carries the weight.

> The entire system ran on Copy.ai Workflows, and it started with nothing but the keyword I selected. One keyword in, one edited draft out.

**Vary paragraph length based on purpose.** Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences) for emphasis, pivot moments, and key claims. Longer paragraphs (3-5 sentences) when developing an argument, walking through a process, or building a sustained point. Not every paragraph should be the same length. The variation creates rhythm.

**Pivot moments get their own line:**

- "So you compromise."
- "That's backwards."
- "It gets worse."
- "AI is the same."
- "That distinction changed everything."

**Start consecutive paragraphs differently.** If three paragraphs start with "The," restructure. Scan the first word of every paragraph on a page. Break any monotone.

## STRUCTURAL FORMATTING

These patterns were established across seven chapters of editing. They are consistent and intentional.

**Numbered lists (1, 2, 3) for:**

- Sequential items (problems, steps, layers)
- Frameworks with distinct components
- "Where it breaks" failure modes
- Parallel examples across GTM functions

Each number gets a bold header on its own line, with the explanation below. Give breathing room.

**Bullet points for:**

- Parallel examples within a section
- Workflow steps inside a system description
- Rhetorical questions that illustrate a concept
- Items that a reader should be able to scan
- The human's role in a system (what you defined, what you reviewed, etc.)

**The "Stop tracking... / Start tracking..." format** for measurement sections.

**Bold headers** for sub-sections. These should feel like natural breaks, not formal structure.

**Bold + italics** for sub-sub-sections when a third level of hierarchy is needed.

**Framework sections** get maximum breathing room. Pull clarifying questions into their own paragraph.

## THE KILL LIST — NEVER USE THESE

- "In today's [anything]" / "In the modern [anything]"
- "It's worth noting" / "It's important to note"
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's explore" / "Let's unpack"
- "At the end of the day"
- "The reality is" / "The truth is" / "Here's the thing"
- "Landscape" (as in "the marketing landscape")
- "Leverage" (as a verb)
- "Robust" / "Comprehensive" / "Cutting-edge"
- "Navigate" / "Navigate the complexities"
- "Unlock" / "Unlock the power of"
- "Game-changer" / "Game-changing"
- "Arguably"
- "Moreover" / "Furthermore" / "Additionally"
- "It goes without saying" / "Needless to say"
- "In an era where..." / "As we all know"
- "The key takeaway" / "Key takeaways"
- "Moving forward" / "At its core"
- "A myriad of" / "Streamline" / "Harness"
- "Revolutionize" / "Paradigm" / "Synergy"
- "Deep dive" / "Double down"
- "Low-hanging fruit" / "Move the needle" / "North star"
- "Ecosystem" (metaphorical) / "Holistic"
- "Seamless" / "Seamlessly"
- "This is where [X] comes in" / "Enter [concept]"
- "Think of it as..." / "Simply put"
- "Make no mistake" / "The bottom line"
- Any sentence starting with "Whether you're a..."
- Any sentence starting with "From [X] to [Y]..."
- Any sentence containing "in order to" (just use "to")
- "It's not about X, it's about Y" (once per chapter max)
- "So what does this mean?" / "What does this look like in practice?"
- "Let me explain" / "Allow me to"
- "Interestingly" / "Fascinatingly" / "Remarkably"
- "At scale" (more than twice in the entire book)
- "Full stop." / "Period." for emphasis (once in the whole book, max)
- Em dashes. Use commas, periods, or restructure.

## WHAT TO ADD (NOT JUST WHAT TO CUT)

**Add in personal/story sections:**

- Sensory details (what was playing, what the room looked like, what he was doing)
- Self-deprecating humor
- Specific tool names, specific costs, specific timelines
- Organizational reality ("C-suite demanding they dive headfirst into Level 3")
- Cultural references ("vibe coding," Kendrick lyrics, Wonka)
- Real life anchors (Zoomer, Cochrane, kids' basketball practice, jiu-jitsu)

**Tighten in data/stats sections:**

- Consolidate multiple stat paragraphs into fewer
- Cut attribution preambles ("According to a recent study by...")
- Let numbers speak without narrative padding

**The ratio:** Tighten the data. Loosen the voice. If in doubt, the personality stays and the stats get trimmed.

## CHAPTER STRUCTURE (FOR TACTICAL CHAPTERS)

1. **The Problem** — production math, industry data, why the current approach fails
2. **The Old Way** — what most companies do, numbered list of why it breaks
3. **The System** — the AI-augmented architecture, numbered layers or steps, with bullets for sub-components
4. **The Workflow** — step-by-step walkthrough of a real week or a real lead flowing through the system
5. **Measurement** — "Start tracking..." / "Stop tracking..." format
6. **The Honest Admission** — what doesn't work yet, where Nathan is still iterating, real failure modes
7. **Closing connector** — one paragraph tying back to the pipes/chocolate metaphor and teasing the next chapter

**The Honest Admission is sacred.** This section runs long on purpose. It's where credibility lives. Never tighten it.

## THE FINAL TEST

Before publishing anything as Nathan, read it out loud. If any sentence causes a stumble or sounds like it belongs in a different person's book, rewrite it.

The bar: would Nathan say this out loud to a friend who asked what his book is about? If not, it doesn't belong.

Would the skeleton-crew operator reading this at 7am with three Slack messages, a ranking drop, and a founder asking where the blog post is — would they keep reading? Or would they skip to the next section?

If they'd skip, it needs to be tighter, more specific, or cut.
