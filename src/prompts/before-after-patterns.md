# Before/After Anti-Patterns — For Writer and Editor Reference

## THE THREE RECURRING PROBLEMS

### 1. Repetitive H2 Structure
Every section follows: general claim → supporting stat → punchy takeaway. By the third section, the reader feels the template.

RULE: Never use the same H2 structure twice in a row. If one section is claim → stat → takeaway, the next must be different: problem → example → implication, or question → answer → complication, or contrast → resolution, or narrative → data → position.

### 2. Repetitive Sentence Construction
Every sentence is the same length and shape: declarative, 10-15 words, period. "X is Y. Z is W. The A is B." It reads like bullet points concatenated into a paragraph.

RULE: Vary sentence length within every paragraph. No paragraph should have four sentences of the same length. Short sentences create emphasis. Long ones build context and momentum.

### 3. Lack of Personality
Generic statements that take no position: "Audio is highly effective at building familiarity." "The depth of engagement is actually growing across the B2B landscape."

RULE: Take a position. Instead of "highly effective," show WHY with a specific comparison. Instead of "actually growing," give the number and compare it to something the reader knows. The writer has an opinion — the language should show it directly.

## SPECIFIC ANTI-PATTERNS TO FLAG

1. **H2 → intro sentence → bullet list → done**: This is a listicle section, not an essay section. Needs prose development.

2. **Mirror-image sections**: If the Podcast section and the Webinar section have identical skeletons (just different nouns), restructure one of them.

3. **Setup sentences**: "This makes audio perfect for capturing broad awareness" tells the reader what the next sentence will say. Cut it and lead with the actual claim.

4. **Simple question → numbered list**: If the answer is yes or no with context, write it as prose in 2-3 sentences. Numbered steps are for actual processes that require sequence.

5. **Hedged takes**: "highly effective," "actually growing," "is a flawed premise" — these are hedged. A confident writer says: "Engagement is going up, not down. 51 minutes on average. That's longer than most sales calls."

## WHAT THE GOOD VERSION DOES

- Each section earns its own shape
- Sentence length varies deliberately (long setup, short punch)
- The writer takes positions and trusts the reader to follow
- Arguments are developed across paragraphs, not compressed into one sentence
- The Before explains. The After argues.
